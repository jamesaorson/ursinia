1. This chapter records Saul's first military action as king — the relief of Jabesh-gilead from the Ammonite Nahash —
   and ends with the public confirmation of his kingship at Gilgal.
1. The Spirit of God drives the action.
   1. When Saul heard the news of Nahash's threat to gouge out the right eye of every man in Jabesh-gilead,
      "the Spirit of God rushed upon Saul" (v. 6) — the same language used of the judges (Othniel, Samson, Gideon).
   1. This is significant: Saul's earliest kingship looks like the period of the judges. God's Spirit is the source
      of his power, not his office or his own ability.
   1. This is a warning embedded in the text. When the Spirit departs from Saul later (1 Sam. 16:14), he will be
      left with the office but not the power — a king in title only.
1. Nahash the Ammonite is a picture of the enemy's contempt.
   1. His proposed terms (v. 2) — to gouge out the right eye of every man in Jabesh-gilead as a disgrace to all
      Israel — are designed not merely to defeat but to humiliate and disfigure God's people publicly.
   1. This is how the enemy always works: not content with harm, but aiming at shame and permanent marking.
   1. God's deliverance through Saul reverses the shame completely — the men of Jabesh-gilead are saved whole, and
      Saul's response to those who wanted to execute his detractors is generous: "Not a man shall be put to death
      today, for today the Lord has worked salvation in Israel" (v. 13).
1. The renewal of the kingdom at Gilgal points to the significance of place and covenant.
   1. Gilgal was where Israel first crossed into the land under Joshua, where they were circumcised and where the
      reproach of Egypt was rolled away (Josh. 5:9).
   1. Renewing the kingdom there is a deliberate act of covenant memory — Saul's kingship is being placed in the
      lineage of Joshua's conquest, in the land of promise, under the God of the covenant.
   1. Yet even here Samuel's words carry an undercurrent of warning (cf. ch. 12). The kingdom is given, but
      faithfulness is required to keep its blessing.
1. The chapter ultimately presents Saul at his best — Spirit-empowered, generous in victory, and rightly giving
   credit to God. It makes the later tragedy of his reign all the more grievous. He knew what Spirit-filled
   leadership looked like, and he walked away from it.
