1. This chapter is Samuel's farewell address as judge — a hinge moment where the old order of judges gives way to the
   new order of kings, and Samuel makes clear that God's covenant demands have not changed with the political structure.
1. Samuel establishes his own integrity before the people.
   1. He invites the people to testify against him: have I taken anyone's ox? Have I defrauded or oppressed anyone?
      (vv. 3–4). The people answer: you have not.
   1. This is not self-congratulation — it is covenant accountability. A leader who enriches himself from his office
      has corrupted his calling. Samuel's clean record is itself a rebuke to what kingship would tend to become
      (cf. 1 Sam. 8:11–17).
   1. Christ is the ultimate fulfillment of this — the one leader of God's people who can be accused of nothing
      (John 8:46), who came not to be served but to serve (Mark 10:45).
1. Samuel rehearses God's saving acts to indict Israel's request for a king.
   1. He recounts the pattern of the judges: Israel sins, God gives them into the hand of enemies, they cry out,
      God sends a deliverer (vv. 9–11).
   1. The demand for a king was a rejection of God as their king — even when Nahash the Ammonite threatened them,
      the correct response was to cry out to God, not to demand a human king (v. 12).
   1. Yet God in mercy grants them a king anyway. This is grace under judgment — God working through Israel's sin,
      not because He approves of it, but because He is sovereign over it.
1. The great call of verses 14–15 and 20–25 is the center of the chapter.
   1. The key is not the form of government — judge or king — but covenant faithfulness: "If you will fear the Lord
      and serve him and obey his voice and not rebel against the commandment of the Lord" (v. 14).
   1. The sign of thunder and rain in harvest time (vv. 17–18) is a covenant sign — God interrupting the natural
      order to confirm the gravity of Samuel's words. The people rightly fear.
   1. Samuel's response to their fear is pastorally rich: "Do not be afraid... the Lord will not forsake his people,
      for his great name's sake, because it has pleased the Lord to make you a people for himself" (vv. 20, 22).
      God's faithfulness is grounded in His own name and purpose, not Israel's merit.
   1. Samuel closes with a personal covenant: "As for me, far be it from me that I should sin against the Lord by
      ceasing to pray for you" (v. 23). Intercession is not optional for the servant of God — to stop praying for
      God's people is itself a sin.
1. The chapter teaches that the covenant framework does not change when circumstances change. New structures, new
   leaders, new political arrangements — none of these alter the basic call: fear God, serve Him, obey His voice.
   And none of them remove the need for faithful intercession.
