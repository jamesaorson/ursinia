1. Chapter 13 is the pivot of 1 Samuel — the height of chapter 11 gives way to the beginning of the end.
1. Saul offers the burnt offering himself rather than waiting for Samuel (v. 9).
   1. He was not authorized to do so for the offering belonged to the priestly office, not the kingly.
   1. His reason (vv. 11–12):
      - The people were scattering and Samuel had not come within the appointed time.
      - Expediency over obedience.
   1. Samuel arrives immediately after (v. 10)  Saul's faith failed.
1. Samuel's verdict is the hinge of the narrative (vv. 13–14).
   1. "You have not kept the commandment of the Lord your God."
   1. The Lord has sought out "a man after his own heart", a phrase we hers later to describe David.
1. There is  great irony when considered in light of chapters 11 and 12.
   1. Saul had just witnessed the covenant renewal at Gilgal. He lacked not information but trust.
   1. He does not repent, but rather he explains. This pattern will deepen through Sayl's reign.
1. Christ is the better king.
   1. Where Saul substituted his own judgment for God's command under pressure,
      Christ in Gethsemane surrendered entirely: "not my will, but yours" (Luke 22:42).
