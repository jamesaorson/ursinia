1. Chapter 13 is the pivot of 1 Samuel — the height of chapter 11 gives way to the beginning of the end.
1. Saul offers the burnt offering himself rather than waiting for Samuel (v. 9).
   1. He was not authorized to do so — the offering belonged to the priestly office, not the kingly.
   1. His reason (vv. 11–12): the people were scattering and Samuel had not come within the appointed time.
      - This is the logic of expediency over obedience.
   1. Samuel arrives immediately after (v. 10) — Saul's faith failed on the very threshold of the appointed hour.
1. Samuel's verdict is the hinge of the narrative (vv. 13–14).
   1. "You have not kept the commandment of the Lord your God."
   1. The Lord has sought out "a man after his own heart" — the first hint of David in the book.
1. This is the great irony when held against chapters 11 and 12.
   1. Saul had just witnessed the covenant renewal at Gilgal. He lacked not information but trust.
   1. He does not repent — he explains. This pattern will deepen in chapters 15 and 28.
1. Christ is the anti-type of Saul here.
   1. Where Saul substituted his own judgment for God's command under pressure,
      Christ in Gethsemane surrendered entirely — "not my will, but yours" (Luke 22:42).
