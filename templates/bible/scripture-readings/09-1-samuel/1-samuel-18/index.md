1. Jonathan's soul is knit to David and he makes a covenant with him, giving David his robe, tunic, sword, bow, and belt (vv. 1–4).
   - Jonathan is Saul's heir. In stripping himself of the royal regalia, he is effectively renouncing his own claim and investing it in David.
   - This is not compelled; it flows from love. Jonathan sees what Israel cannot yet see — that David is the true king.
1. The women's victory song becomes the catalyst for Saul's unraveling: "Saul has slain his thousands, and David his tens of thousands" (vv. 6–7).
   - Saul's response reveals that what he feared most — losing the kingdom — has become his consuming thought (v. 8).
   - The jealousy does not emerge from nowhere; it surfaces what was already there. Saul's kingdom-mindedness was always self-referential rather than God-referential.
1. Saul hurls his spear at David twice while David plays the harp (vv. 10–11).
   - The spirit of distress from God is back (cf. 16:14). What was introduced in chapter 16 now escalates into attempted murder.
   - David is doing nothing except serving Saul, and Saul tries to kill him for it.
1. Saul's jealousy gives way to fear: "the LORD was with David but had departed from Saul" (v. 12).
   - The theological diagnosis is given plainly. Saul is not responding to David; he is responding to the absence of God from himself and the presence of God with another.
   - This phrase — the LORD was with David — recurs three times in this chapter (vv. 12, 14, 28), anchoring every act of David's success.
1. Saul schemes to have the Philistines kill David by offering his daughters as brides with military conditions attached (vv. 17, 21, 25).
   - Merab was promised but given to another (v. 19); Michal is used next as "a snare" (v. 21).
   - The dowry demanded is a hundred Philistine foreskins — a death trap. Saul cannot raise his hand against David openly, so he engineers circumstances to accomplish it indirectly.
1. David delivers two hundred foreskins, doubling the required amount (v. 27).
   - Every device Saul sets against David results in David prospering further. The trap becomes a platform.
   - God's hand on David means that even Saul's evil schemes serve David's advancement.
1. Saul grows more afraid and becomes David's enemy permanently (vv. 28–29).
   - The narrative arc of this chapter is the complete inversion of where it began. Saul begins as king with every institutional advantage; by the end he is afraid of a shepherd who outperforms him in everything.
   - David does not scheme to take the kingdom. He simply serves faithfully, and God gives him everything Saul is trying to keep.
