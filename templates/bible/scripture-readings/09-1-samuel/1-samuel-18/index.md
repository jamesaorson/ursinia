1. Jonathan makes a covenant with David, giving him his robe, tunic, sword, bow, and belt (vv. 1–4).
   - Jonathan is Saul's heir. In stripping himself of the royal regalia, he is renouncing his own claim to the throne.
   - This flows from love for David, for Jonathan sees what Israel does not yet see, which is that David is the true king.
1. The women's victory song is the catalyst for Saul's unraveling, "Saul has slain his thousands, and David his tens of thousands" (vv. 6–7).
   - Saul's response reveals that what he has feared most is losing the kingdom, not a fear of the LORd (v. 8).
   - Saul's kingdom-mindedness was always self-referential rather than God-referential.
1. Saul hurls his spear at David twice while David plays the harp (vv. 10–11).
   - The spirit of distress from God has not left (cf. 16:14) and Saul's sin has escalated to attempted murder.
   - This phrase "the LORD was with David" occurs three times in this chapter (vv. 12, 14, 28).
1. Saul schemes to have the Philistines kill David by offering his daughters as brides with military conditions attached (vv. 17, 21, 25).
   - Saul can no longer raise his hand against David openly, so he engineers circumstances to accomplish it indirectly.
   - David delivers two hundred foreskins, doubling the required amount (v. 27).
     - Every device Saul sets against David results in David prospering further.
     - God's hand on David means that even Saul's evil schemes serve David's advancement.
1. Saul grows more afraid and becomes David's enemy permanently (vv. 28–29).
   - The narrative arc of this chapter is a complete inversion of position.
     - Saul begins as king with every advantage, as recognized king of Israel.
     - By the end, he is afraid of a shepherd who has outperformed him in everything.
   - David does not scheme to take the kingdom, but simply serves faithfully, and God has given him everything to glorify His name.
