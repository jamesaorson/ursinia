1. Jonathan does what Saul should have — he acts in faith without waiting for permission or certainty.
   1. He tells no one but his armor-bearer (v. 1), while Saul sits under a pomegranate tree (v. 2).
   1. His confession is the theological heart of the passage: "Nothing can hinder the Lord
      from saving by many or by few" (v. 6).
      - This is the opposite of Saul's reasoning in chapter 13, where numbers and circumstances
        drove him to disobedience.
1. Jonathan seeks a sign, but from a position of trust rather than doubt (vv. 9–10).
   1. He is not demanding certainty before he acts — he has already committed to going.
      The sign confirms direction, not courage.
1. The Lord routs the Philistines through Jonathan and his armor-bearer alone (vv. 13–15).
   1. An earthquake follows — the language of divine warfare, echoing Sinai and the Psalms.
   1. Israel's army joins only after God has already broken the enemy (v. 20) —
      they inherit a victory they did not begin.
1. Jonathan is the contrast the narrative has been building toward since chapter 13.
   1. Saul's dynasty was rejected because of unbelief dressed as practicality.
   1. Jonathan's faith — simple, bold, theologically grounded — is what kingship in Israel was
      always supposed to look like.
1. The irony is sharp: the son has the heart his father lost.
   1. Jonathan points forward to David, and ultimately to Christ, the king whose confidence
      in the Father never wavered regardless of the odds against him.
