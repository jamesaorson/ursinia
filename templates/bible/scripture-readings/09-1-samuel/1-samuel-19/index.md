1. Saul commands his servants and Jonathan to kill David; Jonathan warns David and intercedes for him before Saul (vv. 1–5).
   - Jonathan argues the case from David's record of service and innocence: David struck the Philistine and the LORD worked a great salvation; why would Saul sin against innocent blood?
   - Jonathan has placed himself between his father's murderous intent and his covenant friend. This is mediation at personal cost.
1. Saul swears an oath that David will not be put to death, and David returns to Saul's service (vv. 6–7).
   - The reconciliation is genuine in form but shallow in root. Saul's hatred is not resolved — only suppressed.
1. After David's next military victory the spirit of distress returns, and Saul tries to pin David to the wall with his spear again (vv. 9–10).
   - This is the pattern: external success, internal unraveling. Saul cannot tolerate David's continued favor with God.
   - The spear is now a reflex — Saul's instrument of false authority turned against the one whom God has actually authorized.
1. Michal helps David escape through a window, places a household idol in the bed, and deceives Saul's messengers (vv. 11–17).
   - Michal is doing for David what Rahab did for the spies — using deception to protect a man under divine protection, at risk to herself.
   - The presence of a household idol (teraphim) in her home is left without comment, but it is not flattering. She protects David, but with divided loyalties. God uses her faithfulness to David despite her unfaithfulness to Yahweh alone.
1. David flees to Samuel at Ramah — his first refuge is the prophet who anointed him (v. 18).
   - When everything collapses, David goes to where the word of God came to him. Samuel is the source of his legitimacy.
1. Saul sends three successive groups of messengers to Naioth, and the Spirit of God comes upon each group and they prophesy instead of capturing David (vv. 20–21).
   - The Spirit does not merely protect David by hiding him. He disarms every agent of Saul's wrath by overwhelming them with prophetic activity.
   - God does not need David to fight his way free. Saul's power structure is overridden without David lifting a finger.
1. Saul himself goes to Naioth and is overcome by the Spirit; he strips off his robes and prophesies all day and night before Samuel (vv. 23–24).
   - The phrase "Is Saul also among the prophets?" appeared in chapter 10 when the Spirit first came upon Saul as a sign of his anointing. Here the same phenomenon returns, but now it functions as restraint and humiliation rather than commissioning.
   - Saul came to capture David and ends up prostrate before the prophet who anointed his replacement.
   - The Spirit of the LORD will not be weaponized against the LORD's anointed. Every power Saul marshals against David turns into an occasion for God's sovereignty to be displayed.
