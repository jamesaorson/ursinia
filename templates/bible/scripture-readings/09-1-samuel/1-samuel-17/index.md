1. Goliath's challenge is a formal one: single combat to decide the battle, with servitude as the stakes (vv. 8–9).
   - Israel does not lack courage — they lack someone who can meet the challenge.
   - Goliath comes forward morning and evening for forty days (v. 16). No one answers.
1. David arrives not as a soldier but on an errand, carrying food for his brothers (vv. 17–22).
   - The one who will answer the challenge was not assembled with the army; he was tending sheep and running bread.
1. David's first question is theological: "Who is this uncircumcised Philistine, that he should defy
   the armies of the living God?" (v. 26).
   - While the soldiers see a military problem, David sees a theological one: defiance of God.
   - This is the same orientation Jonathan showed in chapter 14 — framing the conflict around what God can do,
     not around the odds.
1. Eliab — the one Samuel first mistook for the Lord's anointed — rebukes David: "I know your pride
   and wickedness of heart" (v. 28).
   - Human assessment remains wrong throughout the chapter. The one Eliab dismisses is the one God had already chosen.
   - David's response is measured: "Was it not just a question?" (v. 29). He does not defend himself by revealing
     his anointing.
1. Saul's armor does not fit David (v. 39).
   - "I cannot walk in these — I am not accustomed to them." He removes it.
   - David does not fight Goliath with borrowed equipment. He goes as what he is: a shepherd with a sling.
   - The refusal of Saul's armor is also the refusal of Saul's approach to kingship.
1. The pre-battle exchange carries the theological weight of the whole narrative (vv. 45–47).
   - Goliath curses David by his gods. David answers with a confession:
     "I come against you in the name of the LORD of Hosts...the battle is the LORD's."
   - The purpose of the victory is stated before it happens: "Then the whole world will know that there is a God
     in Israel" (v. 46).
   - This is not David's victory — it is the LORD's, executed through David.
1. David kills Goliath with a single stone from a shepherd's sling (vv. 49–50), then uses Goliath's own sword
   to take his head (v. 51).
   - The enemy is destroyed by his own weapon. The means of death is turned against the one who wielded it.
1. Israel runs toward the enemy only after the champion has fallen (vv. 52–53).
   - The army inherits a victory they did not begin — they rout and plunder because someone else fought first.
1. David's victory is the shape of the gospel.
   - He fights as the representative champion for a people who cannot defeat the enemy themselves.
   - He enters the conflict not with the weapons of the world but with something the enemy despises.
   - His people inherit what he won. He goes first; they follow.
   - The enemy's own weapon turned against him points to Christ, who destroyed death through death,
     rendering powerless the one who held the power of death ([Hebrews 2:14](/bible/versions/bsb/#hebrews)).
