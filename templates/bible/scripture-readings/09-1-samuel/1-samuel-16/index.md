1. God rebukes Samuel's mourning over Saul and sends him to Bethlehem with a mission already decided:
   "I have selected from his sons a king for Myself" (v. 1).
   - The rejection of Saul is not a setback but a step — God's plan for Israel's king was never undone by Saul's failure.
1. Samuel fears Saul will kill him, and God provides a cover: bring a heifer for sacrifice (v. 2).
   - This is not deception — Samuel does sacrifice. The cover is a partial disclosure, not a lie.
   - God is not naive about the political danger, and he navigates it without requiring Samuel to be reckless.
1. Samuel arrives and immediately repeats Israel's mistake.
   - When he sees Eliab — tall, presumably impressive — he says: "Surely here before the LORD is His anointed" (v. 6).
   - This is exactly the reasoning that produced Saul. Samuel has not yet internalized the lesson of chapter 8.
1. God's rebuke reorients the entire selection: "the LORD does not see as man does. For man sees the outward
   appearance, but the LORD sees the heart" (v. 7).
   - Seven sons of Jesse pass before Samuel. Not one is chosen.
   - The word of the LORD is not a close call; human categories of fitness simply do not apply.
1. The chosen one is out tending the sheep (v. 11) — not summoned for the occasion, not standing in the lineup.
   - Jesse did not even think to bring him. David is the overlooked eighth son.
   - The king after God's own heart was not where human eyes would look for a king.
1. Samuel anoints David, and immediately "the Spirit of the LORD rushed upon David from that day forward" (v. 13).
   - The anointing and the Spirit arrive together — this is the shape of kingship as God intended it.
1. The departure of the Spirit from Saul is not subtle: "a spirit of distress from the LORD began to torment him" (v. 14).
   - What was given is withdrawn. The contrast with David's anointing is immediate and structural.
1. David enters Saul's service as a musician and armor-bearer (vv. 21–22).
   - The servant recommended to Saul is described as "a mighty man of valor, a warrior, eloquent and handsome,
     and the LORD is with him" (v. 18).
   - Saul receives exactly the one God had chosen for him — without knowing it.
   - David's lyre temporarily drives away the spirit of distress (v. 23), giving Saul rest he no longer deserves.
1. The anointing of David is the pivot of the book.
   - "Mashiach" — anointed one — is being conferred on the overlooked shepherd son from Bethlehem,
     whose anointing by the Spirit points forward to the one described as despised and rejected by men
     ([Isaiah 53:3](/bible/versions/bsb/#isaiah)), anointed by the Spirit at his baptism
     ([Luke 3:22](/bible/versions/bsb/#luke)), and appointed king not according to human expectation
     but according to the Father's purpose.
