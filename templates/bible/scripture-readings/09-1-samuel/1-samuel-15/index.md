1. The command is total and unambiguous: devote Amalek to destruction — people, livestock, everything
   (v. 3).
   1. The herem against Amalek is rooted in Exodus 17:14 — a word God had sworn after the unprovoked
      attack on Israel in the wilderness. This is not arbitrary; it is the fulfillment of a covenant
      curse decades in the making.
   1. Saul is given the specific task of executing God's long-standing judgment. There is no
      interpretive room left.
1. Saul obeys partially (vv. 8–9).
   1. He destroys what is "despised and worthless" and keeps what is "good" — Agag, and the best
      of the livestock.
   1. Saul has edited God's command according to his own sense of what is worth keeping. This is the
      same logic as chapter 13: circumstances and judgment substituted for obedience.
1. God's word to Samuel is grief before it is verdict (v. 11).
   1. "I regret that I made Saul king, for he has turned back from following me."
   1. This is not a reversal of God's decree — it is the language of genuine divine grief over human
      sin, the same pattern we see in Genesis 6:6. God is not surprised; He is not pleased.
1. Saul meets Samuel with self-congratulation: "I have performed the commandment of the Lord" (v. 13).
   1. The sheep and oxen are lowing in the background as he says it (v. 14).
   1. His defense — that the animals were spared for sacrifice (v. 15) — dresses disobedience in
      the language of worship. He has reframed his failure as piety.
1. Samuel's rebuke is the theological center of the chapter (vv. 22–23).
   1. "To obey is better than sacrifice, and to listen than the fat of rams."
   1. Sacrifice is not the end; obedience is. The offerings God commanded were themselves acts of
      obedience — they were never meant to replace it. Saul has inverted the order entirely.
   1. "Because you have rejected the word of the Lord, he has also rejected you from being king."
      The symmetry is exact: the measure of his rebellion is the measure of his loss.
1. Saul's response reveals what drove him from the start (v. 30).
   1. "I have sinned; yet honor me now before the elders of my people."
   1. He wants absolution without transformation — the rite of reconciliation without the reality of
      it. His concern is his standing before men, not his standing before God.
1. Samuel completes the herem Saul refused (v. 33).
   1. "Samuel hacked Agag to pieces before the Lord at Gilgal."
   1. The prophet does what the king would not. The office of king had not removed the prophetic
      word — it remained above the throne.
1. Samuel and Saul part permanently (v. 35). The narrative is over before David has even appeared.
   1. "Samuel did not see Saul again until the day of his death."
   1. The Spirit will depart Saul and come upon David — God's purposes cannot be frustrated by a
      king's disobedience; they simply move around him.
1. The obedience that is better than sacrifice finds its fulfillment in Christ alone.
   1. Where Saul substituted ritual for compliance, Christ offered both — the sacrifice that was
      also perfect obedience, from the manger to Gethsemane to the cross.
   1. He is the king "after God's own heart" that Samuel's verdict was already reaching toward.
