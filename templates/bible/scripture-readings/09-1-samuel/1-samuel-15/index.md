1. God commands total destruction of Amalek — people, livestock, everything (v. 3).
   - The herem against Amalek is not arbitrary; it fulfills the judgment God swore in Exodus 17:14
     after Amalek attacked Israel in the wilderness. Saul is given the task of executing a
     long-standing covenant verdict.
1. Saul obeys partially, destroying what is "despised and worthless" while keeping Agag and the best livestock (vv. 8–9).
   - He has edited God's command according to his own judgment of what is worth keeping.
   - This is the same pattern as chapter 13: circumstances and Saul's own reasoning substituted for obedience.
1. God's word to Samuel is grief before it is verdict: "I regret that I made Saul king, for he has turned back from following me" (v. 11).
1. Saul meets Samuel with self-congratulation: "I have performed the commandment of the Lord" (v. 13).
   - The sheep and oxen are lowing in the background as he says it (v. 14).
   - His defense — that the animals were spared for sacrifice — dresses disobedience in the language of worship.
1. Samuel's rebuke is the theological center of the chapter: "To obey is better than sacrifice, and to listen than the fat of rams" (v. 22).
   - Sacrifice was never meant to replace obedience — it was itself an act of obedience.
   - "Because you have rejected the word of the Lord, he has also rejected you from being king." The symmetry is exact.
1. Saul's response reveals what has driven him throughout (v. 30).
   - "I have sinned; yet honor me now before the elders of my people."
   - His concern is his standing before men, not before God. Absolution without repentance.
1. Samuel completes the herem Saul refused, hacking Agag to pieces (v. 33), then parts from Saul permanently (v. 35).
   - The prophet does what the king would not. The prophetic word remains above the throne.
1. Christ is the king after God's own heart that Samuel's verdict was reaching toward.
   - Where Saul substituted his judgment for God's command and dressed it in piety, Christ obeyed perfectly,
     offering the sacrifice that was also perfect obedience (Phil. 2:8).
