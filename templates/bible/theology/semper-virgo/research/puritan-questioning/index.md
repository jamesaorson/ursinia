# Puritan Questioning and Rejection of Mary's Perpetual Virginity (*Semper Virgo*)

## Introduction

While the major Protestant Reformers (Luther, Calvin, Zwingli) generally affirmed or tolerated the traditional
doctrine of Mary's perpetual virginity, many 17th-century **Puritans** and closely aligned Reformed theologians
increasingly questioned or denied the post-partum aspect of the doctrine. They viewed it as lacking clear scriptural
warrant, classifying it as a non-essential "vain doctrine" or pious opinion at best, rather than an article of faith.
This shift reflected stricter *sola scriptura* principles, anti-Catholic polemic, and a high view of ordinary
marriage.

The doctrine was not formally condemned in major confessions (e.g., Westminster Standards are silent on it), but key
Puritan figures actively challenged its necessity or truth.

## Key Examples of Puritan/ Reformed Questioning and Denial

### 1. Gisbert Voetius (1589–1676)

- Dutch Reformed scholastic theologian, highly influential on English Puritans and Westminster divines.
- In *Syllabus of Theological Problems* (1643):
  **"Whether she remained for the whole of life in that state of virginity? It is denied. It is permitted to be
  investigated."**
- Voetius affirmed virginity before and during birth but explicitly rejected lifelong perpetual virginity as
  unproven, allowing open biblical inquiry.

### 2. Samuel Rutherford (1600–1661)

- Scottish Puritan, Westminster Assembly divine, and major covenant theologian.
- In *The Divine Right of Church Government* (1646):
  He lists the **perpetual virginity of the virgin Mary** among **"these vain doctrines... not in Scripture... the
  vain and sapless doctrines of men, and will-worship."**
- Rutherford tolerated it as a possible historical opinion in some writings but firmly rejected elevating it to
  doctrine.

### 3. Matthew Poole (1624–1679)

- English Puritan biblical commentator, author of *Annotations Upon the Holy Bible*.
- On Matthew 1:25:
  **"There are no cogent arguments to prove that Mary had no more children by Joseph... The Holy Ghost had made use
  of the virgin for the production of the Messias; why after this her womb should be shut up, and Joseph take her
  home to be his wife, and not use her as such I cannot tell... marriage being God's ordinance, and the undefiled
  bed honourable."**
- Poole dismissed traditional defenses of perpetual virginity as unnecessary and left the question open while
  clearly leaning against it.

### 4. Matthew Henry (1662–1714)

- Classic Puritan pastor and commentator (*Exposition of the Old and New Testaments*).
- On Matthew 1:25:
  **"It is certain that it cannot be proved from scripture... Dr. Whitby inclines to think that... afterwards... he
  lived with her, according to the law."**
- Henry stated plainly that perpetual virginity lacks scriptural proof and cited approvingly the view that Joseph
  and Mary consummated their marriage normally afterward.

## Broader Puritan Stance and Implications

- Many Puritans treated *semper virgo* as a matter of **pious opinion** or **human tradition** rather than divine
  faith (e.g., William Perkins: "we make not this opinion any article of our belief").
- They often revived or tolerated the ancient **Helvidian view** that the "brothers" of Jesus were full siblings
  from Mary and Joseph.
- **Reasons for questioning**:
  - Literal reading of "until" (Matt 1:25) and "firstborn" (Luke 2:7).
  - Emphasis on the honorableness of marriage and conjugal duty.
  - Rejection of anything perceived as unnecessary Catholic accretion.
- This Puritan-era de-dogmatizing paved the way for widespread denial in 18th–19th-century evangelical
  Protestantism.

## Conclusion

The Puritan revocation was not usually a fierce polemic but a quiet yet firm shift: from early Reformation tolerance
to viewing perpetual virginity as unscriptural speculation. It illustrates the gradual triumph of strict biblicism
over patristic consensus in Protestant Mariology.
